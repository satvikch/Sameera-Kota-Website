## ORIGINALITY POLICY — APPLIES TO ALL CONTENT

**Every word produced for this project must be 100% original.** This policy is absolute and applies to all content — website copy, procedure descriptions, meta tags, structured data, blog posts, FAQs, testimonials, and any other text.

### What Constitutes Plagiarism (All Prohibited)
- Copying text verbatim from any source
- Paraphrasing (rewriting someone else's sentences with synonyms)
- Closely adapting structure and phrasing from another website
- Using another site's content as a "starting point" and modifying it
- Replicating a competitor's procedure descriptions with word substitutions
- Copying medical explanations from health portals (WebMD, Healthline, NHS, Medscape, etc.)
- Using manufacturer marketing copy for devices, laser platforms, or mesh products

### What Is Acceptable
- Using common medical facts that are public knowledge (e.g., "the gallbladder stores bile produced by the liver")
- Using standard medical terminology (e.g., "laparoscopic cholecystectomy", "inguinal hernia")
- Being inspired by the STRATEGY of a reference (e.g., "they organise procedures by body system" is a strategy — the actual category names, descriptions, and content must be original)
- Quoting published surgical guidelines (EAES, SAGES, ASI) with attribution, for the practice to verify

### Why This Matters for a Medical Website
- **Google YMYL policy:** Medical websites face the strictest quality standards. Duplicate content triggers penalties
- **Copyright law:** Website text is copyrighted. Copying it is infringement regardless of whether it's a small clinic or a large hospital
- **Professional reputation:** A surgeon's website with copied content undermines trust
- **SEO damage:** Duplicate content across sites causes both to rank lower

## Content Voice & Tone

### Brand Personality
Dr. Sameera Kota's practice voice is: **Reassuring. Evidence-based. Patient-first. Warm but precise.**

This is a surgical practice where patients often arrive anxious — facing piles, a hernia, gallstone pain, or a lump they are worried about. The hierarchy is:
1. Safety and clinical expertise
2. Evidence-based, minimally invasive approach
3. Patient comfort, dignity, and informed consent
4. Fast, honest recovery expectations

### Voice Attributes
| Attribute | Do | Don't |
|-----------|----|----|
| **Authority** | Reference qualifications (FMAS, FIAGES), laparoscopic training, case volume | Use "best", "top", "#1" — these are unverifiable claims |
| **Empathy** | Acknowledge that surgical concerns are frightening and often embarrassing (proctology especially) | Be clinical and detached — patients are people, not cases |
| **Honesty** | Set realistic recovery expectations, name the risks, explain why a procedure is or isn't right | Promise painless miracles, hide complications, minimise recovery |
| **Accessibility** | Lead with plain language, add medical terms in parentheses | Lead with jargon, assume patients know surgical terminology |
| **Restraint** | Let evidence and outcomes speak for themselves | Use aggressive marketing, fear-based urgency, guarantee-of-cure language |

## Content Patterns by Type

### Procedure Descriptions
**Summary (card view):** 20-35 words. Lead with what the patient is struggling with, end with the approach. Never start with "We" or "Our".

**Bullets (card view):** 3 short phrases. Concrete patient-facing benefits or facts (incision size, discharge time, recurrence rate, return-to-activity).

**Disclaimer (card view):** 1 short sentence. Candidacy or caveat ("Ideal for Grades 2 & 3", "Clinical assessment required", "Technique tailored to size and location").

**Full description (modal or expanded view, when present):** 3-4 sentences.
- S1: What condition + why patients seek help
- S2: How the approach works (simplified, original explanation)
- S3: What distinguishes Dr. Sameera Kota's approach (minimally invasive, laser-based, experience)
- S4: Expected outcome with honest caveats

### Doctor Bio
- P1: Credentials and specialisation (MBBS, MS, FMAS, FIAGES; laparoscopy and laser proctology)
- P2: Philosophy and approach to care (patient-first, minimally invasive, informed consent)
- P3: What patients experience (warm consultation, clear explanation, clinical rigour)
- Maximum 150 words. Must sound human, not like a CV.

### Headlines
- Hero: 6-10 words. Patient outcome focused.
- Section H2: 4-8 words. Standalone meaning.
- Avoid cliches: no "unlock", "reveal", "transform", "journey", "love your body"

### FAQs
- Questions written as real patients ask (conversational)
- Answers start with direct response, then elaborate
- 2-4 sentences per answer
- Never condescending

### Testimonials
- **ALWAYS mark as templates** when not real — never present fabricated text as real patient words
- Format: problem → hesitation → experience → result → recommendation
- Under 50 words each
- Include procedure name (e.g., "Laser Piles", "Gallbladder", "Hernia")
- Proctology testimonials should preserve patient dignity — first names or initials only

### CTAs
- Warm and low-pressure: "Book a Consultation", "Speak With Us", "Schedule Your Visit", "Online Consultation"
- Never aggressive: no "Act Now!", "Don't Wait!", "Limited Slots!"
- Emergency copy (for appendicitis, acute abdomen) can be more directive but still calm

## Medical Content Guidelines

### Procedure Descriptions — Accuracy Rules
- Describe the science in original words from first principles
- Don't name specific device or mesh brands unless the practice confirms
- Honest recovery windows — never minimised
- Never claim cures for chronic conditions (e.g., hernia "repair", not "cure"; piles can recur — be clear about recurrence risk)
- Include "results may vary" or equivalent for any outcome claim
- Candidacy matters: some procedures are right only for certain grades/sizes/presentations — say so
- No off-label claims; no fabricated statistics
